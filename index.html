Professional Profile

Brahim Brahmi (Senior Member, IEEE) received the Ph.D. degree in engineering from École de Technologie Supérieure (ÉTS), Montreal, QC, Canada, in 2019, specializing in nonlinear control and robotics.

He is currently an Assistant Professor with the Electrical Engineering Department, KFUPM, Dhahran, Saudi Arabia, and also a member of the Interdisciplinary Research Center for Intelligent Manufacturing and Robotics. His research focuses on nonlinear and adaptive control, bio-robotics, rehabilitation robots, industrial automation, the Internet of Things (IoT), motion control for nonholonomic and underactuated systems, haptics, autonomous systems, intelligent systems, and machine learning.

Development and Evaluation of a Physics-Driven VR FEM Training Platform for a Novel Surgical Spine Procedure

From a biomechanical perspective, spinal surgeries are among the most complex surgical procedures due to the highly intricate and nonlinear mechanical structure of the human spine. Recent advancements in biomechanics, computational simulation, and finite element method (FEM) modeling have enabled researchers to gain valuable insight into the forces, stresses, and interactions involved in spinal surgical procedures. These developments have also facilitated the use of FEM-based tools for preoperative planning and, more recently, for physics-driven virtual reality (VR) surgical training.

Medical device companies continuously introduce novel surgical procedures that advance the state of the art in spine surgery. One such advancement is the introduction of minimally invasive techniques for single-segment lumbar discectomy and fusion, the most frequently performed spinal surgical procedure. While this updated approach significantly reduces cost and patient recovery time, it also presents increased technical complexity, requiring intensive surgeon training before safe and confident independent clinical practice can be achieved.

This project focuses on the development and evaluation of a high-fidelity, physics-driven FEM-based VR training platform for this new surgical approach. The platform integrates biomechanically accurate spine models with immersive virtual reality environments to provide realistic procedural simulation and quantitative feedback during surgical training.

Nonlinear Control of a Seven-Degrees-of-Freedom Exoskeleton Robot for Active and Passive Rehabilitation Tasks

This project focuses on the development of a seven-degrees-of-freedom upper-limb exoskeleton robot, known as the ETS-MARSE robot, for the rehabilitation of patients with upper-limb impairments. The work addresses the design, implementation, and validation of a human-inspired inverse kinematics solution and robust nonlinear control strategies to enable both passive and active rehabilitation motions in the presence of kinematic and dynamic uncertainties, as well as unexpected external disturbances.

With the rapidly growing population of post-stroke patients, there is a critical need to improve access to high-quality physiotherapy through advanced robotic rehabilitation technologies. Rehabilitation robots have gained significant attention due to their ability to deliver intensive, repetitive, and long-duration therapy, while providing quantitative performance data that allow clinicians to accurately assess patient progress.

The primary contributions of this project are: (i) the development of a human-like inverse kinematics framework that generates smooth and natural rehabilitation motions, and (ii) the design of robust nonlinear control algorithms capable of maintaining high tracking accuracy and stability despite uncertain dynamics and external disturbances.

Design and Development of a Smart Robotic Platform for Hospital Assistance

The global health crisis that emerged in 2020 exposed the vulnerability of healthcare systems when confronted with unexpected and high-risk situations, highlighting the urgent need for safer and more resilient hospital operations. While medical interventions must continue even during crises, healthcare professionals are often exposed to significant risks, particularly in infectious or high-contact environments.

This project aims to support the modernization of healthcare services in Saudi Arabia through the design and development of a smart robotic platform for hospital assistance. The proposed system integrates mechanical design, embedded control, and artificial intelligence (AI) within a unified framework to assist healthcare staff while minimizing direct physical contact with patients.

The system is based on a mobile manipulator robot equipped with autonomous navigation, safe human–robot interaction capabilities, and AI-driven perception. By leveraging Internet of Things (IoT) connectivity and edge–cloud computing, the robot can process real-time data, make autonomous decisions, and perform assistance functions such as delivering medications, meals, and medical supplies.

The proposed platform is expected to enhance operational safety, efficiency, and precision while addressing workforce shortages and the growing demand for high-quality healthcare services. This work directly supports Saudi Vision 2030 objectives in digital transformation, healthcare innovation, and robotics technology development.

Internet-Based Remote Kinesthetic Therapy Using an Intelligent Rehabilitation Robot

This project proposes the development of the Agentic Clinical Policy Search (ACPS) system—an intelligent, adaptive, and safety-critical platform designed to advance internet-based tele-rehabilitation using robotic therapy. ACPS integrates large language models (LLMs), robotic control, and augmented reality (AR) to enable clinicians to deliver personalized kinesthetic therapy to patients remotely while maintaining strict safety guarantees.

A core contribution of ACPS is its ability to automatically generate, verify, and deploy robot control policies tailored to individual patients. AI-generated control actions are continuously monitored by a built-in safety verification engine, which enforces physical and clinical constraints before and during execution to ensure patient protection at all times.

To address privacy, latency, and scalability challenges in tele-rehabilitation, the project introduces SEMO-Edge, a compact on-device learning module that enables each rehabilitation robot to adapt locally to patient progress without relying heavily on cloud computation. This edge-based architecture preserves patient data privacy while maintaining real-time responsiveness and safety.

The ACPS framework will be validated through a multi-stage evaluation process, including high-fidelity simulations, experiments with healthy volunteers, and small-scale clinical trials. By combining verified AI decision-making, real-time adaptation, and remote clinical supervision, this project aims to accelerate patient recovery, reduce therapist workload, and establish new standards for safety, transparency, and trust in AI-driven rehabilitation robotics.

Adaptive Learning Control Design for Safe and Efficient Human-Robot Collaboration

Human–robot collaboration (HRC) is becoming a cornerstone of modern manufacturing and dynamic industrial environments; however, achieving safe, efficient, and intuitive interaction remains challenging due to system instability, overly conservative safety constraints, and limited adaptability to individual human behaviors. This project proposes an adaptive learning control framework that integrates advanced stability-guaranteed control with preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) to enable safe, flexible, and personalized human–robot collaboration.

Unlike conventional passivity-based controllers that excessively restrict robot dynamics to ensure safety, the proposed control strategy allows controlled non-passive energy exchange while rigorously preserving system stability through Lyapunov-based analysis. This reduces conservatism and improves assistive performance during physical human–robot interaction. To address variability in human behavior, a novel PbRL paradigm is developed to incorporate human preferences directly into the learning process, enabling rapid adaptation without extensive retraining or reliance on predefined demonstrations.

The proposed methods are validated on a 6-DOF robotic manipulator equipped with force/torque and physiological sensing, and evaluated through simulation and real-world collaborative tasks such as precision assembly and object handover. Performance is benchmarked against rigid safety limits, classical passivity-based controllers, and rule-based learning approaches using metrics including stability, task efficiency, force smoothness, and user satisfaction. This work advances the state of the art in collaborative robotics by bridging stability-guaranteed control with human-centered learning for intelligent manufacturing applications.

Multifunctional Robotic Assistive Arm (mR2A) for Activities of Daily Living Assistance

Loss of upper and/or lower extremity function due to aging, stroke, spinal cord injury, and neurological disorders significantly limits independence and quality of life for millions of individuals, particularly wheelchair users. This project focuses on the design, development, and validation of a multifunctional robotic assistive arm (mR2A) to support activities of daily living (ADLs) and mobility assistance for elderly individuals and individuals with upper/lower extremity dysfunctions (IwULED).

The proposed mR2A is a modular, lightweight robotic manipulator that can be mounted on powered wheelchairs or fixed/mobile bases and equipped with a set of interchangeable, task-specific grippers. Unlike existing assistive robotic arms that rely on a single general-purpose gripper, the mR2A employs a novel automatic attachment–detachment mechanism and a magazine of specialized end-effectors, enabling efficient execution of a wide range of ADL tasks such as feeding, drinking, object manipulation, door opening, and personal care.

A unified adaptive control framework, incorporating force sensing, electromyography (EMG), and intelligent impedance control, enables intuitive human–robot interaction while accommodating varying levels of user impairment. The system is complemented by a smartphone-based user interface that allows task selection, performance monitoring, and remote assistance.

The project follows a user-centered design methodology informed by extensive customer discovery, clinical input, and focus groups, and includes rigorous experimental validation with healthy participants and individuals with disabilities. By improving independence, reducing caregiver burden, and enhancing mobility and self-care, the mR2A represents a significant advancement in assistive robotics for home and community use.
